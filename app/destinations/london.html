Destination London

London, United Kingdom is home to Red Hat UK Ltd., located at 28 Dorset Square, London, NW1 6QN — in the elegant Marylebone district of central London, a neighborhood of Georgian townhouses, garden squares, and beautifully preserved 19th-century character just minutes from Paddington Station, Regent's Park, and the West End. London is one of the world's great financial and technology capitals, home to the European operations of the majority of the world's largest technology companies, and Red Hat's UK office serves one of the most technically sophisticated and cloud-mature enterprise markets in Europe. UK enterprises have been early and enthusiastic adopters of OpenShift and Ansible — a national preference for automation that says a great deal about the British character.

Steps from the Dorset Square office, The Providores and Tapa Room on Marylebone High Street is one of London's most celebrated and inventive restaurants — New Zealand-born Chef Peter Gordon's pioneering fusion of Pacific Rim ingredients with European technique has earned it consistent acclaim and a loyal following since 2001. For a truly special evening, The Ledbury in Notting Hill holds two Michelin stars and is considered one of London's finest dining experiences — Chef Brett Graham's extraordinary cooking with British ingredients is among the best in the country. The Marylebone Farmer's Market on Sunday mornings is also one of London's finest.

Visiting the Red Hat London office means operating from one of the world's most culturally inexhaustible cities — where the Tate Modern, the British Museum, the Southbank, Hyde Park, and more Michelin-starred restaurants than any other city on Earth are all within reach of a 20-minute Tube ride. London's open source and cloud-native community is exceptional, with Red Hat events drawing some of the largest enterprise technology audiences in Europe. An afternoon walk from the Marylebone office through Regent's Park and back is one of the most restorative ways to think through a difficult architecture decision. Mind the gap, embrace the queue, and drink the tea — it genuinely helps.
